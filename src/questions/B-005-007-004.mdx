## The Concept: Radio Frequency Ranges and Classification

Radio frequencies are systematically divided into ranges to help organize and understand electromagnetic spectrum usage. These ranges are defined by their frequency boundaries and have specific characteristics that determine their propagation, applications, and communication capabilities.

The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) standardized these frequency ranges to facilitate global radio communication. Each range has unique properties that make it suitable for different types of communication, from local broadcasts to intercontinental radio contacts.

### Frequency Range Overview

The standard frequency ranges progress logarithmically:
- Low Frequency (LF): 30 kHz - 300 kHz
- Medium Frequency (MF): 300 kHz - 3 MHz
- High Frequency (HF): 3 MHz - 30 MHz
- Very High Frequency (VHF): 30 MHz - 300 MHz

### Amateur Radio and HF Bands

For amateur radio operators, the HF range is particularly important. These frequencies allow for long-distance communication through a phenomenon called "skywave propagation," where radio waves are reflected off the ionosphere, enabling contacts thousands of kilometers away.

## Real-World Examples

### ✅ HF Frequency Band Characteristics:
- **40-Meter Band**: 7.0-7.3 MHz, where 7125 kHz is located
- **20-Meter Band**: 14.0-14.35 MHz
- **80-Meter Band**: 3.5-4.0 MHz

### ❌ Frequencies Outside HF Range:
- **AM Radio (MF)**: Typically around 1 MHz
- **FM Radio (VHF)**: Around 100 MHz
- **GPS Signals (UHF)**: Around 1.5 GHz
