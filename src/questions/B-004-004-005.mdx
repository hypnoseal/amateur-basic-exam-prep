## The Concept: Field-Effect Transistor Fundamentals

A field-effect transistor (FET) is a three-terminal semiconductor device that uses an electric field to control the flow of electrical current. Unlike bipolar junction transistors that rely on current flow between different semiconductor regions, FETs control current flow through a semiconductor channel by modifying its conductivity.

The three primary electrodes in a FET are:
- **Source**: The entry point for charge carriers
- **Drain**: The exit point for charge carriers
- **Gate**: The control electrode that modulates channel conductivity

Think of a FET like a water channel where the gate acts as a controllable valve. The source is where water enters, the drain is where it exits, and the gate determines how wide or narrow the channel becomes, thus controlling the water (or in this case, electrical charge) flow.

### Charge Carrier Dynamics

In an n-channel FET, electrons are the primary charge carriers. The gate's voltage creates an electric field that either widens or narrows the channel, allowing more or fewer electrons to flow from the source to the drain. The drain is specifically designed as the exit point for these charge carriers, completing the electrical circuit.
